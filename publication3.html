Quantifying the Impact of Data Poisoning: A Case Study on CNN-based Intrusion Detection Systems

IEEE Conference 2025

Research Paper

2025

Cybersecurity, Adversarial ML

Authors

Mahendra Reddy, et al.

▶ Keywords


Data Poisoning

Intrusion Detection

Adversarial Machine Learning

CNN Security

Network Security

Model Robustness

▶ Abstract


This research quantifies the vulnerability of Convolutional Neural Network-based Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) to data poisoning attacks — a class of adversarial manipulation where malicious samples are injected into training data to degrade model performance at inference time. As deep learning models become increasingly relied upon for network security, understanding their susceptibility to training-time attacks is critical for building trustworthy defense systems. We present a systematic case study examining multiple poisoning strategies and their measurable impact on CNN-based IDS accuracy and reliability.


Our experiments evaluate label-flipping, backdoor injection, and gradient-based poisoning techniques across varying contamination ratios on standard network intrusion datasets including NSL-KDD and CICIDS. We measure the degradation in detection rates, the increase in false positive and false negative rates, and the stealthiness of each attack in evading common data sanitization defenses. Results demonstrate that even modest poisoning ratios can cause significant performance degradation, with certain attack strategies reducing detection accuracy by over 25% while remaining undetectable by standard outlier removal methods.


Beyond quantifying the threat, we evaluate the effectiveness of several defense mechanisms including robust training algorithms, spectral signature detection, and activation clustering for identifying poisoned samples. Our analysis provides security practitioners with concrete risk assessments and actionable recommendations for hardening CNN-based IDS deployments against data poisoning threats in real-world network environments.

▶ Methodology


We designed a controlled experimental framework to isolate the effects of different poisoning strategies on CNN-based IDS models. Baseline models were trained on clean datasets to establish reference performance metrics. Poisoned variants were then created by systematically contaminating training data at 1%, 5%, 10%, and 20% ratios using each attack method. All models shared identical architectures and hyperparameters to ensure fair comparison.


Defense evaluations followed a realistic threat model where defenders had no prior knowledge of the specific attack type employed. Each mitigation technique was applied as a preprocessing step or training modification, and its effectiveness was measured by the recovery in detection accuracy relative to clean baselines. Statistical significance testing across multiple random seeds ensured the reliability and reproducibility of all reported results.
