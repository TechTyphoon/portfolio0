RAFL: Resource-Aware Federated Learning with Adaptive Edge Coordination

▶ Abstract


This paper introduces RAFL, a Resource-Aware Federated Learning framework designed to address the fundamental challenge of heterogeneous device participation in distributed machine learning across edge networks. Traditional federated learning approaches assume uniform computational capacity among participating clients, leading to significant inefficiencies when deployed on real-world edge infrastructure where devices vary dramatically in processing power, memory, bandwidth, and energy availability. RAFL dynamically adapts training workloads, model complexity, and communication schedules based on real-time resource profiling of each participating edge node.


The framework introduces an adaptive edge coordination protocol that intelligently schedules local training rounds, adjusts gradient compression ratios, and assigns variable-depth model subnetworks to clients based on their current resource state. A lightweight resource monitor running on each edge device continuously reports CPU utilization, available memory, battery level, and network conditions to the coordination server, enabling proactive workload redistribution that prevents stragglers from bottlenecking global model convergence.


Extensive experiments across heterogeneous device clusters demonstrate that RAFL achieves comparable model accuracy to standard FedAvg while reducing total training time by up to 40% and energy consumption by 35% on resource-constrained devices. The framework maintains data privacy guarantees inherent to federated learning while significantly improving practical deployability in scenarios involving smartphones, IoT sensors, and mixed-capability edge nodes — making privacy-preserving distributed AI viable at scale.

▶ Methodology


RAFL was evaluated on a testbed of 50+ heterogeneous devices including Raspberry Pis, Jetson Nanos, Android smartphones, and cloud VMs simulating diverse edge computing environments. Experiments used standard federated learning benchmarks (CIFAR-10, FEMNIST, Shakespeare) with non-IID data distributions to simulate realistic heterogeneous data scenarios. Each experiment was repeated across multiple random partitions to ensure statistical validity.


The adaptive coordination protocol was compared against FedAvg, FedProx, and other heterogeneity-aware baselines under identical conditions. Resource profiles were both measured from real devices and synthetically varied to test edge cases. Communication costs were tracked at the byte level, and energy measurements used hardware power monitors to capture actual device-level consumption rather than theoretical estimates.
