A Comparative Study of Lightweight Deep Learning Models for Real-Time Object Detection on Edge Devices

IEEE Conference 2025

Research Paper

2025

Deep Learning, Edge Computing

Authors

Mahendra Reddy, et al.

▶ Keywords


Object Detection

Edge Computing

Lightweight CNNs

Real-Time Inference

Model Compression

Embedded Systems

▶ Abstract


This paper presents a systematic comparative analysis of lightweight deep learning architectures optimized for real-time object detection on resource-constrained edge devices. With the growing demand for on-device intelligence in applications such as autonomous navigation, surveillance, and industrial automation, deploying accurate yet computationally efficient detection models has become a critical challenge. We benchmark several state-of-the-art lightweight architectures including MobileNet-SSD, YOLOv5-Nano, EfficientDet-Lite, and TinyML variants across multiple edge platforms such as Raspberry Pi, NVIDIA Jetson Nano, and Google Coral.


Our evaluation spans key metrics including mean Average Precision (mAP), frames per second (FPS), memory footprint, power consumption, and thermal behavior under sustained inference loads. Results reveal significant trade-offs between detection accuracy and inference speed across different hardware-software configurations. We further investigate the impact of model compression techniques — quantization, pruning, and knowledge distillation — on balancing these trade-offs for practical deployment scenarios.


The study provides actionable guidelines for practitioners selecting detection models for specific edge deployment contexts, demonstrating that no single architecture universally dominates. Instead, the optimal choice depends heavily on the target hardware constraints, acceptable latency thresholds, and detection accuracy requirements of the application domain. Our findings contribute a reproducible benchmarking framework that can be extended to evaluate future lightweight architectures as they emerge.

▶ Methodology


We established a unified evaluation pipeline to ensure fair comparison across all models and hardware platforms. Each architecture was trained on the COCO dataset under standardized conditions and subsequently converted to platform-specific optimized formats (TensorFlow Lite, ONNX, TensorRT). Inference benchmarks were conducted under controlled thermal and power conditions with statistical significance testing over multiple runs.


Model compression experiments applied INT8 quantization, structured pruning at varying sparsity levels, and knowledge distillation from larger teacher models. Each technique was evaluated independently and in combination to measure cumulative effects on accuracy degradation and inference speedup. Power consumption was monitored using external measurement equipment to capture real-world energy profiles relevant to battery-powered edge deployments.
